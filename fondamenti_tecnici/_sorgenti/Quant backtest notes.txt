The first pitfall illustrated is **Look-ahead bias**. The example strategy uses alternative data to generate a signal across a basket of equities, ranked by quantiles, implementing a long-short, market-neutral approach based on signal buckets. Initially, the backtest shows a very attractive Sharpe ratio around 3 and a strong monotonic relationship between signal buckets and forward returns, suggesting excellent predictive ability.

**Look-ahead bias is defined as the error of incorporating information into your trading decision that was not available at the time the trade was initiated.** This can be understood with the envelope analogy:  
- Envelope 1: Information accessible at or before decision time  
- Envelope 2: Information available only after the decision point  

Using Envelope 2 data when making decisions causes the backtest to "peek into the future," unrealistically inflating performance.

The video emphasizes that look-ahead bias is deceptively easy to create, especially when dealing with alternative data or complex datasets. Matching dates, timing when data arrives, or normalizing signals with future returns can unintentionally cause this bias. When the presenter controlled for look-ahead bias in the example strategy, the Sharpe ratio plummeted from about 3 to a much lower figure, **indicating a severe degradation in performance once the bias is removed.**

An important insight is that look-ahead bias does not always produce an overly “perfect” backtest. Sometimes it results in a **modestly promising but misleading signal**, where the Sharpe ratio appears realistically positive (e.g., 0.9 or 1.2), yet the strategy is still fundamentally polluted. This subtlety makes look-ahead bias particularly treacherous for quants who may mistake the results for genuine predictive power.

Forms of look-ahead bias can include:  
- Using information from a week ahead to make trading decisions today  
- Using forward returns to normalize signals for past or current decisions  
- Incorporating data without correct timestamp alignment  

To avoid this bias, **all models, parameters, and datasets must be strictly adapted to the filtration of available data at the point of trading decisions**, ensuring no future data leaks into the signal.

The second major pitfall discussed is **Data snooping, overfitting, or P-hacking**. This relates to repeatedly testing numerous parameter combinations or models on the same historical data set and choosing the one that appears best purely by chance or statistical noise, rather than true predictive content.

The example given involves moving average crossover strategies: two moving averages with windows such as (3,5) or (7,12). The (7,12) combination yields a higher Sharpe ratio in-sample, tempting the trader to select it for live trading. However, if many moving average pairs are tested, selecting the “best” pair is a form of **overfitting** the parameterization to past data without finding genuine underlying market structure.

When tested out-of-sample (live trading or on unseen data), this "optimized" (7,12) crossover performs poorly, even showing a negative Sharpe ratio around -1.8. This stark degradation highlights the danger of overfitting models to historic data and then assuming the best in-sample performance represents future returns.

The key lesson: simply optimizing parameters to maximize historic Sharpe without theoretical or economic justification often yields models that do not generalize. The presenter advises **walk-forward validation** as a practical mitigation:  
- Split data into sequential slices (e.g., several months in-sample, several months out-of-sample)  
- Optimize parameters on each in-sample slice  
- Test them on the subsequent out-of-sample data  
- Roll this process forward through time  

This method reduces the likelihood of overfitting and gives more robust parameter sets.
 
An additional indicator of overfitting is **large performance sensitivity to small parameter changes**. If altering parameters slightly (e.g., moving average windows from (7,12) to (8,12)) causes sharp performance drops, the model is likely overfit and unstable.

The third pitfall is **Survivorship bias**, described using the classic WWII plane armor analogy: analysts examined bullet holes on returning planes to determine where to add armor but failed to consider planes that did not return. The mistake was to armor the planes where the bullet holes were seen rather than where the non-returning planes had been hit (unknown to them).

In trading, survivorship bias occurs when backtests exclude firms that went bankrupt or were delisted. This artificially inflates strategy performance because the “graveyard” of failed firms is omitted—analogous to ignoring the planes that got shot down.
a strategy with a strong equity curve and positive Sharpe ratio when excluding bankrupt firms. When incorporating these delisted firms properly, the performance **degrades drastically and sometimes becomes negative**, revealing the true effectiveness of the strategy.

The key conclusion is that excluding failed assets from the backtest creates **an unrealistic "survivor-only" universe** that biases returns positively. To avoid this, at each time $T$ when the model makes a trading decision, the equity universe must be predefined and must include **all firms existing at that time, including those that later fail or delist.**

This equity universe can be:  
- Based on historic indices including delisted firms  
- Defined by market capitalization or other criteria including failed firms  
- Ensured to reflect the true market environment at the trading decision time  

Not doing so results in a misspecified backtest that falsely boosts performance estimates.

### Key Insights and Recommendations

- **Look-ahead bias** causes overestimation by leaking future information into past decisions; always align signal data strictly in time.  
- **Data snooping / Overfitting** results from repeated parameter mining; robust model validation requires out-of-sample and walk-forward testing to ensure stability.  
- **Survivorship bias** inflates historic returns by omitting bankrupt/delisted firms; realistic backtests define the universe dynamically including all entities live at each decision point.  
- **Robust strategies should show stable performance across reasonable parameter variations and rigorous validation.**  
- Model simplicity, economic rationale, and real-time data filtration practices are pillars of robustness.  
- Transaction costs, spreads, and slippage matter but are secondary concerns if core biases are not addressed.
