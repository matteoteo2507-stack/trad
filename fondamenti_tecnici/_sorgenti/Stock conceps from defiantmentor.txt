### Detailed Principles of the Scalping Strategy

  1. **Trade stocks only with high volume**, excluding futures, cryptocurrencies, or commodities.  
  2. **Focus on the 10-period and 34-period moving averages.**

### Principle 1: Stock Selection Based on Volume  
- **High volume stocks are preferred** because they tend to be more volatile and dynamic, essential for making quick profits in scalping.

### Principle 2: Usage of Moving Averages to Time Entries and Exits  
- Chart setup: 1-minute candlesticks on a daily chart using Yahoo Finance indicators.

  - Blue line: 34-period moving average (longer-term trend).

- **Short sell signals occur when the pink line crosses below the blue line on downward trends.**  

- However, these signals are imperfect and can produce **false positives or lag in volatile conditions** (examples provided). Hence moving averages alone are insufficient.

### Principle 3: RSI Indicator for Momentum Confirmation  
- The **Relative Strength Index (RSI)** measures momentum and demand intensity for a stock.  
- RSI ranges interpreted as:  
  - Above 75: overbought (potential sell signal).  
  - Below 25: oversold (potential buy signal).  
- RSI helps confirm moving average crossovers by indicating if the stock is due for a price reversal or continuation.  
- Examples show trades in oversold/overbought zones with corresponding price trends validating the strategy.
 
### Principle 4: Profit and Loss Caps at 1%  
- To **minimize emotional trading and risk**, strict caps of 1% profit and 1% loss per trade are enforced.  
- This prevents overexposure during unpredictable price movements and maintains disciplined execution.

### Trading Directions and Meaning  
- USD/JPY at 150 means 1 USD buys 150 JPY.  
- **Going long USD/JPY means betting the yen will weaken versus the dollar (higher USD/JPY rate).**  
- **Going short means the opposite: yen strengthens and USD/JPY falls.**

### FX Trading Methods  
- **Short-term trading based on technical indicators (similar to scalping):**  
  - Use 10- and 34-period moving averages and RSI adapted for FX charts.  
  - Use inverse charts as needed for clarity.  
  - ETFs exist to facilitate FX exposure.  
  
- **Trading via monetary and fiscal policy analysis:**  
  - Compare interest rates and central bank balance sheets between two countries.  
  - Higher interest rates and lower liquidity tend to strengthen a currency; opposite leads to weakening.  
  - Go long on currency with tightening monetary policy and short the one easing policy.

- The Fed uses these tools to control market liquidity — interest rates to influence borrowing costs and the balance sheet to manage money supply.
 
**Implications of Federal Reserve Interest Rate Changes**  
- **When interest rates are low:**  
  - Stock market valuations (Price to Earnings ratios, EV/EBITDA multiples) tend to increase.  
  - Explanation: The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC, nicknamed "whack") is lowered due to reduced cost of debt and risk-free rate component in cost of equity.  
  - A lower discount rate increases present value of future cash flows, leading to higher company valuations despite unchanged earnings.  
  - Example calculation:  
  $$\text{Value} = \frac{\$100}{1 + r}$$  
  where \(r\) is WACC. A decline in \(r\) from 10% to 8% raises the present value from \$90.9 to \$92.6.  
  - Lower interest rates on bonds cause investors to shift capital from bonds to higher-yielding stocks, increasing stock demand and price.  
  - Companies with significant debt benefit from lower interest costs, facilitating expansion and growth; they are more incentivized to borrow.  
  - Growing companies with debt benefit most.  

- **When interest rates are high:**  
  - The opposite occurs: increased cost of capital, lower valuations, potential shift of investors back into bonds.  
  - Companies with strong cash flow and profits fare better under such conditions, rather than high-debt growth companies.  

**Federal Reserve Balance Sheet Effects**  
- **Increasing balance sheet (Quantitative Easing - QE):**  
  - Fed buys Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities from financial institutions, injecting cash.  
  - Increased liquidity makes borrowing easier and cheaper due to surplus cash eager to be lent.  
- **Decreasing balance sheet (Quantitative Tightening - QT):**  
  - Fed sells assets or lets bonds mature, withdrawing liquidity from the market.  
  - Leads to higher borrowing costs and reduced credit availability.

- Macro data are **correlated and not independent**, so understanding macro conditions requires integrating multiple metrics.  
- There is an overwhelming volume of economic data; investors must **prioritize a subset to avoid overload**.  
- Data often have multiple versions (e.g., government vs. institutional methodologies), but the focus here is on the **official versions aligned with Fed monitoring**.  

The video’s scope and methodology are clarified:  
- Explanation centers on 10 **key macroeconomic indicators** chosen for their broad coverage (~80-90%) of economic activity.  
- Focus remains on **official data releases**, as the Fed bases its decisions primarily on these.  
- Implications discussed are limited to **single data points’ market effects**, without delving into extensive indirect or combined impacts.  
- For practical investment application, the presenter refers viewers to a separate stock investing strategy video.  
- Data sources recommended include **Yahoo Finance** and **Investing.com** with alert notifications for real-time updates.  

The discussion begins with GDP:  
- **GDP (Gross Domestic Product)** is the aggregate output indicator — the sum of **Consumer spending (C), Government spending (G), Investments (I), and Net Exports (X)**.  
- Recent GDP growth has been moderate (2%-3%). However, **GDP itself is a lagging outcome, less actionable than the underlying constituent data** released periodically.  
- These underlying data are grouped into three Fed-monitored categories:  
  1. **Job market-related data**  
  2. **Inflation-related data**  
  3. **Economic activities-related data**

The presenter begins detailed explanations starting with **Job Market Indicators**:  

**Unemployment Rate**:  
- Definition: Percentage of unemployed workers actively seeking jobs against total workforce.  
- Interpretation:  
  - **Low unemployment** indicates companies are selling well and likely hiring.  
  - The Fed and government treat **4% unemployment as full employment**, though this is a subjective target without hard scientific backing.  
- Market views on unemployment:  
  - >5.5%: Weak economy  
  - 4%-5.5%: Moderate economy  
  - <4%: Strong economy  
- Fed’s responses:  
  - **High unemployment → expansionary monetary policy (rate cuts or quantitative easing)** to stimulate growth.  
  - **Low unemployment → contractionary policy (rate hikes or tapering)** to cool potential overheating and inflation.  

**Initial Jobless Claims**:  
- Represents weekly **new filings for unemployment insurance** — a **leading indicator of unemployment rate**.  
- Normal range: 250,000 to 350,000 claims weekly; above 350K signals strain, below 250K signals strength.  
- Fed’s policy response parallels unemployment rate:  
  - **High claims → expansionary**  
  - **Low claims → contractionary**  
- The presenter reiterates no single indicator fully determines Fed policy; broader context matters.  

**Non-Farm Payrolls**:  
- Reflects monthly change in employment excluding farming (due to farming being highly cyclical and a small employment share).  
- Interpretation:  
  - 50K–250K jobs added per month is normal.  
  - >250K signals strong job market.  
  - <50K suggests weakness.  
- Fed’s response is inverse to unemployment/inital claims:  
  - **High payroll additions → contractionary policy** due to perceived strong economy.  
  - **Low payroll additions → expansionary policy** to boost employment.  

The video moves to **Inflation Indicators**:  

**Consumer Price Index (CPI)**:  
- Core measure of inflation reflecting price changes in a basket of consumer goods and services compared year-over-year (preferred) or month-over-month.  
- Year-over-year CPI is favored because it smooths seasonal volatility and aligns better with Fed’s 2% inflation target.  
- Inflation interpretation:  
  - Around 2%: Normal inflation  
  - >2%: High inflation  
  - <2%: Low inflation  
- Fed policy responds:  
  - High CPI → contractionary  
  - Low CPI → expansionary  

**Core CPI**:  
- CPI excluding volatile food and energy items to give a more balanced inflation picture.  
- Fed uses this for policy decisions due to reduced distortion from external shocks (geopolitical events, natural disasters).  
- Mechanism and policy implications are the same as total CPI.  
  
**Producer Price Index (PPI)**:  
- Measures inflation from producers’ perspective—price changes of manufacturing goods before reaching consumers.  
- PPI is a **leading indicator for CPI**, providing early insight into future consumer inflation.  
- Interpretation month-over-month:  
  - 0% to 0.2% is normal inflation  
  - Above or below this indicates high or low inflation respectively  
- Fed responds to PPI movements similarly to CPI and core CPI by adjusting monetary policy accordingly.  
- Also noted is the **Core PCE Index** (*not included in the main 10*) which the Fed prefers, as it covers broader expenses paid on behalf of consumers and adjusts for changing behaviors, making it a more stable inflation gauge.  

The video continues with **Economic Activity Indicators**:  

**ISM Manufacturing PMI**:  
- From Institute of Supply Management; a monthly survey of 300-400 manufacturing executives.  
- Measures new orders, production, employment, and inventories.  
- Values interpretation:  
  - 50: Neutral (no change from previous month)  
  - >55: Strong expansion  
  - <50: Contraction  
- Manufacturing represents roughly 10-12% of US economy but is a leading indicator for broader economic trends.  
- Fed responds to strong expansions by tightening policy, and loosening in contractionary phases.  

**ISM Non-Manufacturing PMI**:  
- Covers service sectors (~70% of US GDP) such as education, retail, financial services, hospitality.  
- Price levels in services are stickier due to labor cost components.  
- Similar scale and interpretation as manufacturing PMI.  
- Fed closely monitors this for inflation outlook and policy calibration.  

**Chicago PMI**:  
- Similar to ISM Manufacturing PMI but focused on the Midwest region, a manufacturing hub.  
- Acts as a leading indicator predicting the national ISM Manufacturing PMI 1-2 months ahead.  
- Shares same interpretation scale for economic conditions and Fed responses.  

**Consumer Confidence Index**:  
- Monthly survey of 5,000 U.S. households assessing sentiments on income, employment, and business conditions.  
- Interpretation:  
  - 100 = neutral confidence  
  - 100-120 = moderate confidence  
  - >120 = strong confidence  
  - <100 = weak confidence  
- Fed views strong confidence as signal of a potentially overheated economy and may tighten policy; weak confidence suggests easing is needed.  

The presenter reiterates key caveats:  
- **No single data point determines Fed policy.** Recent Fed decisions illustrate that even with strong job markets and high inflation, other factors like political dynamics and holistic data views weigh heavily.  
- Economic indicators are usually helpful, but **unforeseen events (e.g., COVID-19 pandemic) can disrupt interpretations**, necessitating flexible, updated views.  
- Although 10 indicators are emphasized, **other data may become critical at different times**. For example, CPI’s importance surged post-2022.  
- Mastery of macroeconomic data interpretation **comes gradually with persistent monitoring and practice**.

- **Official data releases aligned with Fed monitoring should be prioritized over alternative data versions** to better anticipate monetary policy changes.  
- **Fed policy reactions generally operate along expansionary vs. contractionary responses, linked to thresholds in job market strength, inflation levels, and economic activity.**

- **Leading indicators (Initial Jobless Claims, PPI, PMI data) offer early signals of future economic conditions and CPI trends, enabling proactive investor positioning.**
